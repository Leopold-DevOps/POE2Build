Chapter I

Build Overview

You are a witch who pays for her spells in blood, curses everything that moves, and then detonates those curses in chains of chaos explosions. The louder your heartbeat, the harder you hit.

The core of this build is a marriage of two mechanics that were, almost accidentally, made for each other. Hexblast is a chaos spell that consumes a curse on its target and explodes — but it suffers from a spellcaster's classic problem: a low base critical hit chance. Blood Mage is a Witch ascendancy whose signature node, Sunder the Flesh, sets the base critical hit chance of your spells to a flat 15% — one of the largest single-node damage transformations available to any caster. Bolt those together and Hexblast stops being a slow utility nuke and becomes a crit engine.

But the deeper synergy — the one this build is actually designed around — is what crits do for a Blood Mage. Gore Spike scales your critical damage bonus off your maximum life, including life overflowing above your maximum from Life Remnants. That inverts the usual caster logic: instead of life being the tax you pay for not dying, life is your primary damage stat. Every life node, every life roll on gear, every remnant you pick up is simultaneously defense and offense.

Chapter II

The Engine — Why This Works

This is the chapter that makes it your build. Understand the engine, and every future patch note becomes something you can evaluate yourself instead of waiting for a guide update.

Principle 1 — One stat, three jobs

Most builds split their budget: some nodes for damage, some for defense, and the two compete. Here, maximum life triple-dips. It is your survival buffer, it is the fuel Sanguimancy spends when you cast, and through Gore Spike it is a direct multiplier on your critical damage. When one stat serves three jobs, every point of investment is worth roughly three times what it looks like on paper. This is why the passive tree in Chapter V looks more like a bruiser's tree than a caster's — that is not a compromise, it is the whole point.

Principle 2 — Fix the crit floor, then multiply

Critical hits are a multiplication problem: chance × bonus damage. Hexblast's native crit chance is poor, which normally makes crit investment on it a waste. Sunder the Flesh replaces that weak base with a flat 15% — meaning every "increased critical chance" modifier you take is now multiplying a big number instead of a tiny one. Once chance is respectable, Gore Spike inflates the bonus side using your life pool. You are attacking both halves of the multiplication with stats you wanted anyway.

Concept — Why crits beat raw damage here

A 15% base crit chance means crit modifiers on the tree and gear have somewhere to grow toward reliably critting on most casts. And because Hexblast fires in slow, heavy casts rather than a machine-gun stream, each individual hit carries a huge share of your DPS — the variance of crits matters less, and their multiplier matters more. Slow heavy hitters are the natural home of crit scaling.

Principle 3 — Curses are "more" multipliers in disguise

Hexblast deals chaos damage, so Despair (which lowers enemy chaos resistance) is your fuse of choice. Resistance reduction is deceptively strong: taking a boss from 30% chaos resistance to 0% doesn't add 30% damage — it multiplies your damage by roughly 1.43×, because you're recovering the portion the boss was deleting. Reduction below zero is even better. Nothing on the passive tree gives you a multiplier that cheap. This is also why the build never "wastes" a curse: Hexblast requires one to detonate, so your damage buff and your trigger are the same button.

Principle 4 — Tempo: fresh curses hit harder

At current patch, Hexblast's quality bonus scales with the remaining duration of the curse it removes. Read that carefully: it rewards you for detonating immediately, not for letting curses sit. The optimal rhythm is a tight two-beat — curse, blast, curse, blast — like a heartbeat. This single line of gem text defines the build's feel, and it's the kind of detail a copied build list never explains.

Principle 5 — Recovery in layers, because leech got humbled

The 0.5 patch cycle overhauled leech: no more instant recovery, and each leech instance is capped. Builds that leaned entirely on Vitality Siphon for sustain got hurt. So this design treats recovery as three independent layers, any one of which can carry a bad moment:

  • Life Remnants — your primary engine. They spawn on kills and periodically on hit, so they flow during mapping and boss fights, and they overflow your maximum.
  • The life flask — deliberately kept strong. Blood Mages spend life constantly, so flask charge generation and recovery rate mods are worth more to you than to any other caster.
  • Leech — now the third layer, not the first. Still worth having via Vitality Siphon later, but sized as a supplement.
Blood Mage — the overflow trick

Life Remnants can push you above maximum life (up to roughly 150% of it). Because Gore Spike counts that overflow, entering a boss phase topped-up-past-full is a genuine damage cooldown. Before a big damage window, sweep up remnants deliberately — you are not just healing, you are charging your crits.

Chapter III

Ascendancy Path

Eight points, taken in the order that solves your biggest current problem — not the order a spreadsheet says maximizes level-90 DPS.

OrderNodeWhy, in this build's logic
Foundation Sanguimancy Free to allocate at current patch. Grants Life Remnants and gives your skills a life cost equal to their mana cost. This is the identity node — the loop in Chapter II does not exist without it. Take it the moment you ascend.
Points 1–2 Sunder the Flesh 15% base spell crit. The single largest transformation available to Hexblast, and it makes every crit node you take afterward worth taking. Damage first, because early on your life costs are small and dying to a life pool problem is rare.
Points 3–4 Gore Spike Crit damage scaled by your life. On its own it's good; after Sunder the Flesh it's a multiplier on a multiplier. From here on, the passive tree's life wheel purchases are damage purchases.
Points 5–6 Whispers of the Flesh The pick that makes this design distinct. Enemies you curse have a chunk of life reserved — effectively a sizeable "more damage" multiplier against every target, because your rotation curses everything before it dies. And cursed targets lose their life regeneration entirely, which quietly deletes a whole class of annoying regenerating rares and boss recovery phases. A pure-crit template undervalues this; a curse-detonation build gets 100% uptime on it for free.
Points 7–8 Crimson Power or Vitality Siphon Crimson Power (energy shield on your body armour counts as extra maximum life) if your gear budget is growing — it turns the biggest ES slot in the game into a life slot, feeding Gore Spike. Vitality Siphon (spell leech) if you're still feeling squishy and want the third recovery layer online. Post-0.5, I lean Crimson Power: leech was demoted, and the build's philosophy is that life is damage.
Patch-sensitive

Blood Mage nodes have been renamed, reordered, and rebalanced multiple times across early access (Sanguimancy went from mandatory-and-costly to free; remnants went from crit-triggered to hit-triggered). Verify node names and values on your in-game tree — but the ordering logic above (identity → crit floor → crit ceiling → curse multiplier → life conversion) holds regardless of exact numbers.

Chapter IV

Skill Gems & Supports

Two buttons carry the build. Everything else exists to make those two buttons safer, faster, or bigger.

Primary — Hexblast, the detonation

Hexblast unlocks around character level 41 (a level 11 uncut skill gem). Until then you level with a different chaos core — see Chapter VII. Once it's online, support it toward one goal: the biggest possible single crit, because your explosion chain copies whatever your hit was.

  • Crit chance support — first slot, always. It multiplies against the 15% base from Sunder the Flesh.
  • Crit damage / heavier-hit support — the second half of the crit multiplication.
  • Area of effect support — grows each explosion in the chain; on a 10-explosion cap, AoE is effectively pack-wide overlap.
  • Cast speed support — Hexblast is a slow, weighty cast, and your rhythm is two spells per cycle. Cast speed compresses the whole heartbeat, which is both damage and safety.
  • Fifth slot: flexible — chaos damage, exposure/penetration effects, or a defensive utility if a boss is punishing your cast animation.
Patch-sensitive

Support gem names and numbers move every major patch, so this list is written as roles rather than a fixed six-link string. In-game, sort supports by tag (Critical, Area, Speed, Chaos) and slot the strongest of each role. If two candidates compete, favor the one whose bonus is a "more" multiplier over an "increased" one.

Secondary — Despair, the fuse

Your curse of choice for damage: it lowers chaos resistance, which Chapter II established is the cheapest multiplier you can buy. Support it for area of effect (curse the whole pack in one cast — the explosion chain needs multiple hexed targets to jump between) and curse effect (deeper resistance reduction). Since patch 0.2, Hexblast cannot lean on aura-style curse application — the manual two-beat is the intended design, so invest in making the curse cast feel good rather than trying to automate it away.

Concept — the defensive swap

Because your damage trigger is "any hex," you own a free defensive mode: swap Despair for Enfeeble (enemies deal less damage) or Temporal Chains (enemies slowed) in content that outpaces your gear. You lose the resistance multiplier but keep the full detonation loop. Very few builds can change their defensive profile by swapping one gem; curse-detonation builds can.

The supporting cast

  • A movement/repositioning skill — non-negotiable. Your cast rhythm plants your feet; blink out between beats.
  • A spirit buff for damage or defense — whatever persistent buff your spirit budget affords; prioritize anything that adds flat damage to spells (it scales with Hexblast's high added-damage effectiveness) or shores up life.
  • A single-target curse setup for bosses — same Despair, but you can keep a second, differently-supported copy (curse effect + duration) for long fights if your sockets allow.
Chapter V

Passive Tree Priorities

No node-by-node list here — that would be somebody else's homework, and it would be stale in one patch. Instead: the shopping list, in priority order, that lets you path it yourself on the live tree.

Priority 1
Maximum Life %
The triple-dip stat. Take life wheels a bruiser would take. Target: enough that a big map hit takes under half your pool.
Priority 2
Spell Crit Chance
Multiplies the 15% base. Take these until crits feel routine, then stop — chance has diminishing returns near the cap.
Priority 3
Crit Damage Bonus
Stacks with Gore Spike. Once chance is respectable, every point here is pure multiplication.
Priority 4
Chaos / Spell Damage
Your bread-and-butter "increased" nodes. Good value early, diluted later — front-load them, respec out if crowded.
Priority 5
Curse Effect & AoE
Curse effect deepens Despair's resistance cut (a hidden more-multiplier); AoE widens both the curse and the explosion chain.
Priority 6
Life Recovery & Flasks
Flask charge gain, recovery rate, remnant-adjacent effects. This is the layer that replaced leech after 0.5 — do not skip it.

How to path like a designer

When two routes cost the same points, prefer the one passing through life. When a jewel socket is one point off-path, take it — jewels are how you retune the build per patch without repathing. Cast speed is your quality-of-life stat: sprinkle it in whenever a node is cheap, because it accelerates the entire two-beat rhythm rather than just one spell.

Blood Mage — the budget rule

A useful self-check as you level: your unreserved life should always comfortably absorb several consecutive casts plus one nasty hit. If casting twice while getting clipped by a boss slam would kill you, you have over-bought damage. Sell some crit, buy some life — remember that on this build, that purchase is barely even a damage loss.

Chapter VI

Gear & Stat Priorities

Shop like the engine, not like a generic caster. Life first on every slot, crit on the slots that can roll it, and one deliberately excellent flask.

Weapon / Focus
Spell damage, spell crit chance, cast speed, flat chaos damage to spells. Hexblast's added-damage effectiveness is high, so flat damage rolls punch above their weight here. This slot is your biggest raw damage lever.
Body Armour
Early: maximum life and resistances. With Crimson Power allocated: the highest energy shield roll you can afford, because it converts into extra maximum life — this is the slot where the ascendancy choice reshapes your shopping. The unique robe The Covenant is thematically on-build (it grants Life Remnants and life-cost casting) but partially redundant with your ascendancy; judge it by its raw ES/life against your rare options.
Helmet · Gloves · Boots
Life, resistances, attributes. Boots want movement speed above almost everything — your rhythm plants your feet, so the time between casts is when you live or die. Gloves can carry cast speed.
Rings & Amulet
Life, flat chaos to spells, crit chance/bonus, resistances, and chaos resistance for yourself. Chaos res is doubly relevant: you fight in melee-adjacent range more than most casters because of remnant pickup.
Life Flask
Treat this slot as gear, not an afterthought. Recovery rate, charge generation, instant-recovery-when-low style mods. You are the class that spends life on purpose; your flask is a weapon system.
Jewels
Life % + crit multi is the dream combination — both halves feed Gore Spike's math. Jewels are also your patch-insurance: when numbers shift, re-rolling jewels is cheaper than re-pathing trees.
Patch-sensitive

Unique item recommendations age fastest of all build content — items get reworked, and the trade economy revalues them every league. The rare-item stat priorities above are the durable part. Before buying any unique, ask: does it feed the loop (life, crit, curse, remnants)? If not, a well-rolled rare is probably better.

Chapter VII

Leveling Roadmap

Hexblast arrives at level 41. The plan before that: level with the chaos toolkit that shares your tree, so the switch costs nothing.

Acts 1–2 · The apprentice (levels 1–~25)

Level as a standard chaos witch: Essence Drain + Contagion (or the strongest chaos damage-over-time pair available at your patch) is the classic engine — Contagion spreads your damage across packs, which teaches you the same "prime the group, then trigger it" instinct Hexblast will demand later. Path your tree toward the life and chaos/spell damage clusters you'll want permanently; nothing you take for this phase is wasted.

Complete your first ascendancy trial in Act 2 the moment it's reasonable. Take Sanguimancy and start living with life costs early — the habit of watching your life orb while casting is the build's core motor skill, and it's better learned on small numbers.

Act 3 → level 41 · The initiate

Pick up Despair as soon as it's available and weave it into your rotation ahead of your damage — you're rehearsing the two-beat before the blast exists. Second ascendancy: Sunder the Flesh. Your DoT leveling core doesn't crit much, so you'll feel this less now than at 41 — that's fine, it's pre-investment.

Level 41 · Ignition

Engrave Hexblast from a level 11 uncut gem (you need roughly 95 intelligence — check you're on track around level 38). Slot crit chance and AoE supports first. The moment it's socketed, your rotation becomes the real one: Despair → Hexblast → move → repeat. Expect an adjustment day: the rhythm is slower per cast but far heavier per hit than DoT leveling.

Maps · The blood mage in full

  • Third and fourth ascendancy trials: Gore Spike, then Whispers of the Flesh, per Chapter III.
  • Gear passes: after each trial, do a shopping pass on the slots in Chapter VI — the ascendancy nodes change what your gear is worth (Crimson Power especially).
  • Difficulty tuning: if maps start hurting, swap Despair for Enfeeble before touching your tree. Curse-swapping is your cheapest difficulty dial.
Chapter VIII

Common Mistakes & FAQ

Every one of these is a mistake the engine predicts. If you understand Chapter II, none of them will surprise you.

I cast Hexblast and it barely did anything

Almost always: the target wasn't cursed. Hexblast without a hex is a fraction of the skill — the curse is not a buff, it is the ammunition. Retrain the two-beat: the curse cast always comes first, every cycle, no exceptions. If you find yourself skipping it on weak packs, that's actually fine — but never skip it on anything you care about killing.

I keep killing myself with my own casts

Three checks, in order. First: are you casting while already low, without remnants on the ground? The life cost is a fixed tax — pay it from a full wallet. Second: is your flask actually good, or is it the one you found in Act 2? Chapter VI says treat it as gear. Third: have you over-leveled your gem relative to your life pool? A higher-level gem costs more life; sometimes running the gem one level lower for a while is the correct, unglamorous fix.

Why not just play energy shield like other witches?

Because the ascendancy pays you to bleed. Gore Spike reads your life, Sanguimancy spends your life, remnants refill your life — an ES-primary Blood Mage is fighting her own class. The one place ES enters this build is through Crimson Power, where your body armour's ES is converted into more life. The engine always routes back to blood.

Should I wait for the curse to tick down before detonating?

No — the opposite. At current patch, quality on Hexblast rewards the remaining duration of the curse it consumes. Fresh curse, immediate blast. If you learned Hexblast intuitions from the first game or old patch notes, this is the one to unlearn.

My single-target feels weak on bosses

Bosses are where the multipliers earn their keep, so audit them: Is Despair actually landing and staying on the boss (bosses can shrug curse duration)? Is Whispers of the Flesh allocated — its life reservation and regen-shutdown are disproportionately a bossing tool? Are you walking over remnants before your damage windows to enter them overflowing (Chapter II's overflow trick)? Fix those three before buying gear.

How do I know if a patch broke or buffed this build?

Read patch notes against the loop, not against the numbers. Ask four questions: Did the crit floor move (Sunder the Flesh)? Did life-to-damage conversion move (Gore Spike)? Did remnant generation or overflow move (Sanguimancy)? Did curse application or Hexblast's detonation rules move? If all four survive, the build survives — the numbers just resize. If one breaks, Chapter II tells you which replacement mechanic to hunt for. That's the entire point of understanding the engine instead of copying a list.

Is this build actually meta?

Honest answer: at the current patch, Blood Mage sits mid-tier in community rankings — the 0.5 leech changes made it more gear-dependent than the top league starters. But this build was never designed to be a tier-list clone; it's designed so that its power comes from a loop you understand and can re-tune every patch. Mid-tier with mastery beats S-tier on autopilot, and the ceiling — overflowing life pools feeding chained crit detonations — is genuinely high once gear arrives.
